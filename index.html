Professional Philosophy

As an academic librarian, I am devoted to supporting a library's environment so that its patrons achieve the best results. My professional philosophy is based on the idea that knowledge and education are fundamental human rights, and a well-established collection is crucial to enshrining those rights. For this reason, I aim to work as a collection development librarian to support faculty, staff, students, and community members in their learning goals.

Library patrons should feel as though they are seen and their interests and needs are reflected in the collection for their academic institution. I will strive to ensure that all patrons feel comfortable knowing they can use the facility to gather their materials, learn, and come together to study. In doing so, I will strongly encourage user experience focus groups to ensure that I am developing the library's collections accordingly.

The vision of collection development should be improved continuously. Frequent study of patron usage of library facilities will help me create a dynamic library that fits every patron to the best of my ability. I am a staunch believer that electronic resources are beneficial, especially to distant patrons, so I want to work with my library's budget to ensure there are enough funds to allocate for digital programs and e-books for my patrons. However, I believe that physical collections are just as valued in any library, and that it is my duty to guarantee that physical collection items are available and at an appropriate amount for those who may benefit from their accessibility.

My goal as a collection development librarian is to continuously improve my collection so that I may do my part in educating the future citizens of our society.

After graduating from the University of North Texas, I plan to work and gain experience working in Texas for some time as an academic librarian. Whether this is in a small community college, or a large and expansive private or state university, I plan to work towards providing services for students' search for information.

1. gateway knowledge

Throughout my academic career, I have developed a strong foundation in the ethics necessary to operate in a way that successfully represents what it means to be a librarian. My understanding of the general knowledge of librarianship has been shaped by my years at the University of North Texas, along with my practicum experience in the Digital Scholarship Lab in the Texas Tech library. During my education, I learned about the different types of libraries and how each has historically supported their communities. My written communication skills have improved with the experience of writing graduate-level essays, presentations, and research papers, which I have completed every semester I have been enrolled in. While enrolled in INFO 5300 Management of Information Agency, I have learned how libraries protect their patrons privacy and the library in totality. During my practicum experience, I have learned and seen the impact of the preservation of historical documents, and the impact that it may have on the patrons that access that information, allowing me to bring the documents that are eligible for open access readily available  while also ensuring that I took the steps necessary to avoid violating copyrights. I have learned about the importance a library plays in a local community, the importance of cultural competency to encourage frequent engagement, and the importance of closing the digital divide to ensure that older patrons are not left behind in the ever-changing technological world. My education has led me to be a strong supporter of local libraries, to voice opposition to censorship attempts, and to encourage people to use the library to continue their learning.

One assignment that I have done that exemplifies my proficiency in gateway knowledge is my research paper on cultural competency that I completed for INFO 5000 Information & Knowledge Professions. This paper highlighted the importance of an active library to a particular community it serves. The specific topic that I had chosen for this paper highlighted that a great library caters to its public and learns what type of people that are in that community, what their demographics are, what kind of events they like, and the types of books they enjoy reading. I felt it was essential to cater certain events to the language and culture within the community to entice more activity between local patrons and their library. This assignment homed in on the first portion of this competency, which is employing the foundational principles of the library profession.

5. Organization of recorded knowledge and information

A librarian needs to understand the trends and stay up to date with them in organizing information so that it may be presented in a beneficial way for patrons and users. I gained this skill and applied it during nearly every course I have been enrolled in for this degree. This was done throughout my time at UNT, as I was taught about understanding the systems and the evaluative skills needed by Dr. Enoch, and I was taught about metadata standards by Dr. Oksana Zavalina. Additionally, I was taught about how cultural biases impact not only collections but also patrons and staff members, with Dr. Vassilieva, and indexing and classification standards with Dr. Kim, working with digital libraries. To tie it all together, during my practicum, I was using these skills to create metadata accordingly and was able to create documents that students and other patrons can use online. All these pieces of this degree have given me the tools to gain the skills of organization of knowledge and information that I need to apply to my future career. These courses helped me become more well-rounded in acting in the library, but there is one that stands out that I believe truly solidified my abilities to work well in the library  and that is maintaining the system of metadata and indexing. These two skills have helped me work successfully during my practicum and in a real-world environment.

A specific assignment for which I utilized this skill was from my introduction to metadata course. This assignment was to create metadata for a painting and an article, and we were given the opportunity to create metadata using Extensible Markup Language (XML) or HyperText Markup Language (HTML). The specific assignment I am using as this example is the second assignment we were given for the class, which used the Metadata Object Descript Schema (MODS) for our Metadata creation record. Dr. Zavalina led us through creating these records through different schemas throughout the semester but always made it a point to explain that not every schema is appropriate for every document. In this specific assignment, Dr. Zavalina allowed us to experiment with this schema and see if we felt that MODS was more accurate and appropriate for an oil painting. This assignment helped exemplify that I excelled with this skill because I could have my XML code work appropriately for this document, meaning that I had coded it correctly and without any issue.

4. Mangement and administration

The management and administration core competency is more than just leading a team to success. It is about being a team player, careful planning, open-mindedness, and creativity. This was apparent to me while taking Management of Information Agency, Information Resources Development, and Information & Knowledge Professions. These three courses explain that being a library manager is much more than ensuring that librarians are available; there are patrons who are using the library, and there are enough books being checked out. Collection development skills, interpersonal skills, and a strong sense of community are some important skills that need to be employed to effectively meet this requirement. While I have not been fortunate enough to be in a managerial position in a library setting, my education has prepared me to listen and work with a team to run a library efficiently and beneficially. Management of Information Agency taught me that there are many different approaches to everyday issues, especially those that might be experienced by a public library. This is important because, while I am not currently pursuing a public library position, knowing how to approach these events is still important because the future is still unknown. Information resources development was also a crucial course to learn about collection development. This skill could be used as a collection development librarian or as someone who manages the budget for a library. As previously mentioned, in my coursework in Information & Knowledge Professions, we discussed the importance of having a strong relationship with the community for which it serves.

The assignment that correlates with this core competency is the gap assignment that I did for my information resource development course. I was given an imaginary budget and instructed to use several book-selling websites to purchase materials for my chosen library. I had previously chosen the Texas Tech University library to write a profile earlier in the semester, so this is the library that I chose for this assignment. I was instructed to search the Texas Tech library and see if there were any gaps in the materials that were missing or had a sparse collection. After my research, I found that the Texas Tech library had a sparse collection of language learning materials. So I used my imaginary budget to purchase $2,000 worth of materials for language learning. I was instructed to create an Excel spreadsheet that showed the author, title of material, publisher, publication date, ISBN/ISSN, format, the source from which I selected, the quantity, and the price of the document. Ms. Coyle instructed us that this process is crucial for cultivating an up-to-date and accurate collection.

8. Social justice

Social justice has many approaches and many faces. Social justice can be marching in the streets for a protest, holding groups and clubs for marginalized groups, and participating in a pride event. This competency requires that a librarian understand their cultural identity and how it may relate to social justice and privilege, and contribute to a climate that supports justice, equity, diversity, and inclusion. As a member of the LGBTQ+ community, I believe that it is imperative to be sure that my community knows that my library space is safe for them to come and be their authentic selves. It is essential to me to be able to provide resources, books, and events to people in the LGBTQ+ community to ensure accessibility and inclusion for those who feel society as cast aside. Libraries are information centers with a long and storied history of maintaining access to books and documents that many want pulled from shelves or censored and are more difficult to access. I have met this exact scenario in INFO 5300, where as a class, we had to decide at what point do we censor documents and books that may be considered inappropriate or mature. This allowed me to consider this real-world problem I can face in my future career. While there is no correct answer for this complicated situation, it was an excellent opportunity to consider what my actions would be in order to act in a way that is in accordance with the ALAs core value, my personal ethics, and serving my community to the best of my ability.

The Omeka project that I completed for INFO 5740 with Dr. Jeonghyun (Annie) Kim is the assignment that correlates with this competency. This assignment was to create my digital repository or library and catalogue it accurately. There was no specific collection or subject that I had to focus on; I was free to choose whichever subject I felt would work for this assignment. I was enrolled in this course for Summer 2024. Since I was enrolled during June, I decided to create a digital repository for politicians who are also members of the LGBTQ+ community. This was incredibly important to me since this was during Pride month. As a member of the community and an aspiring librarian, I wanted to bring to light that these individuals are proof that change can happen. Voices can be heard by those who feel as though they are alone. They also proved that people in the community can make something of themselves and be pillars of nations. I completed this repository with photos in the public domain and was sure to include the required rights and cataloging information.
